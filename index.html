About the Project

Project Brief

Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals

Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals is India's first community digital humanities project dedicated exclusively to preserving the endangered food culture and culinary heritage of West Bengal's indigenous communities. The project was conceived and executed during a summer research internship at the Department of Humanities and Social Sciences (HSS), Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST), Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala, under the mother project HS02: Mapping of Regional Cuisines — Food and Cultural Studies.

The archive documents the food traditions of the Santal, Toto, Rajbangshi, Mech, Lodha, Rabha, Garo, Kheria Sabar, Oraon, Gorkha, Limbu, Bhutia, Birhor, and over twenty other communities — traditions that have remained invisible in mainstream Indian culinary historiography, occluded behind an urban, dominant-caste bias that even the most encyclopedic print works reproduce.

The project's WebGIS platform was built through prompt engineering and vibe coding on Emergent AI, an agentic multi-agent development platform. This website was developed using Claude Sonnet 4.6, an AI by Anthropic.

Methodology

How We Built It

🔭
Digital Ethnography
Following O'Reilly's iterative-inductive framework, treating digital sources — YouTube videos, API data, Google Forms responses — not as raw data but as mediated social practices. Christine Hine's internet ethnography methodology guided our multi-register approach.
📡
YouTube API Mining
Deployed the YouTube Data API v3 to extract 154 long-form Santhali recipe videos and 5 Shorts from the Tribe Family Cooking channel — including rare Mahua wine, Red Ant (Kurkut) curry, Frog cooking, and Eel fish preparations.
📖
OCR Extraction
Using Tesseract and EasyOCR in Python to extract 10 exclusive recipes from Joseph Rozario's rare monograph Untouched North Bengal (Darpan Publication, 2015) — including the Mech Dibouli Bidot pork pulao and Toto Yashai Nayako leaf-parcel fish.
👥
Community Crowdsourcing
A structured Google Form designed around the multi-layer framework, building a bridge between digital methodologies and living indigenous communities across West Bengal's tribal belts.
🤖
Emergent AI Vibe Coding
Full-stack WebGIS application built through prompt engineering on Emergent AI's five-agent platform (Architect, Designer, Developer, Integration, Product Manager agents). React + MapLibre frontend, Python FastAPI backend, MongoDB database — 100% test coverage achieved.
🗺
Deep Mapping
Following Roberts (2016), Springett (2015), and Wood (2015), each geocoded entry opens as a vertical cross-section of place — holding spatial, ethnobotanical, folkloric, sacred, medicinal, and 9,000-year historical origin data simultaneously.
📜 The Historiographical Void This Project Addresses

Despite exhaustive works like K. T. Achaya's Indian Food: A Historical Companion (1994) and Chitrita Banerji's Eating India (2007) — two landmark texts in Indian culinary historiography — there is virtually zero documented data regarding the vernacular foodways of West Bengal's indigenous communities. The complex foraging ecologies, zero-waste ethnobotanical survival foods, and sacred agrarian offerings of Bengal's forest belts remain entirely unindexed in mainstream culinary literature.


The Pratichi Institute and Asiatic Society's 2018 survey of 1,000 tribal households documents the urgency: 31% face food scarcity, the average life expectancy is 58 years (12 below state average), and forest degradation has replaced diverse ethnobotanical diets with PDS-rationed white rice, creating measurable nutritional deficits. This project responds to that emergency with a digital archive designed to preserve irreplaceable knowledge before it disappears entirely.

👨‍🍳
Life After MasterChef India

Over the years, Joseph Rozario has grown as both a chef and a person. He joined a 5-star property — the Renaissance Conventional Centre and Hotel in Mumbai, a prestigious JW Marriott property — where his skills were consistently appreciated by senior chefs. Polishing his culinary expertise at the highest level, Joseph then served as Executive Chef at Hotel Sinclairs, Siliguri, a 3-star property, making him the youngest Executive Chef in North Bengal at the time.


He then found solace in teaching the art of cooking to young people at Modi Public School, a joint-venture culinary arts programme for children. Joseph became a partnership member with Inspiria Knowledge Campus for a Culinary Certification Course for adults, and — recognising his responsibility toward society — associated himself with an NGO in Damdim where he teaches culinary arts to underprivileged children. He endorses the tea brand Riddhi Siddhi Green Tea and has appeared in television commercials for various brands.

📚
His Contribution to This Archive

We are deeply grateful to Chef Joseph Rozario for his extraordinary generosity in responding to our call for help in this — the first initiative of its kind in India — devoted to indigenous food cultural heritage preservation.


His monograph Untouched North Bengal: Cook Book (Darpan Publication, 2015) is the only published comprehensive record of North Bengal's tribal communities' cookbooks and culinary cultures, representing three years of dedicated field research. Through OCR extraction, we recovered 10 exclusive recipes from this rare text — including the Mech Dibouli Bidot pork pulao, the Toto Yashai Nayako leaf-parcel fish, and the Rajbangshi Bhapa steam cake — all of which appear nowhere else in the formal culinary record.


Chef Rozario generously provided a hardcopy of his book at no cost and has expressed willingness to permanently donate it to the IIST Library upon completion of the study. Without him, the North Bengal indigenous culinary layer of this archive would not exist.

Digital Sustainability

How we ensure this archive lasts beyond its creators

Long-Term Preservation

Building a Sustainable Digital Humanities Archive

⚠ The Digital Dark Age: Despite thousands of digital projects launched in the past 20 years, experts warn of a new digital dark age as our ability to produce digital information continues to outpace our capacity to preserve and access that knowledge for the long haul. Most DH projects, dependent on boutique technologies and customised code, have an estimated lifespan of only 10 years before server decay renders them inaccessible.
⚙
The Challenge
For Digital Humanities projects — many based on boutique technologies and customised code — the challenge of future-proofing is particularly pressing. To continue reaping the benefits of these projects, we must invest in workable processes and technologies ensuring user-friendly access for longer than the estimated 10-year lifespan of most current formats.
🏛
The Endings Project Framework
The University of Victoria's Endings Project provides the sustainability framework for this archive. Their multidisciplinary team — including research faculty, programmers, and digital librarians — has developed practical strategies for concluding and preserving scholarly digital projects across a representative range of DH methodologies.

Endings Project Research Questions — and Our Answers

🔚
How do and how should DH projects conclude?
The dynamic nature of digital data lends itself to new possibilities, but projects — even digital ones — need to end. Our archive is engineered with a definitive "completion" state: instead of relying on a continuously running, expensive backend database requiring constant security updates, the final archive will be entirely static — hosted on GitHub Pages with zero database maintenance cost. The project will "conclude" its development phase and enter a stable, maintenance-free preservation phase while remaining fully accessible.
🔧
How should we preserve projects to retain their dynamic features?
By extrapolating from existing theoretical models and our own project implementation, we have developed programming strategies to maintain site usability and functionality. All data is stored as flat CSV and GeoJSON files — readable without any proprietary software. The dynamic map features are handled entirely via client-side JavaScript (MapLibre) and static data files, meaning the site functions perfectly even if downloaded and opened locally on an offline computer.
🗄
Where should projects be archived?
While university libraries are reliable repositories for static digital material, we need multiple repositories that can preserve the dynamic features of projects. Our strategy: GitHub Pages hosting (free, permanent, version-controlled), IIST institutional repository (formal academic preservation), Internet Archive (global distributed backup), and a single-file static package that any library can ingest. The GitHub organisation MappingMemoryMappingMeals serves as the primary institutional anchor, with administrative credentials shared with Prof. Gigy J. Alex at IIST.
📐 Endings Compliance: This archive follows the Endings Project's five-component framework — Data (open standards, version control), Documentation (static files, IP documentation), Processing (reproducible pipelines), Products (no server-side dependence, HTML5/CSS/JS only), and Release Management (persistent URLs, build identifiers, citation instructions). Learn more at endings.uvic.ca/about.html.

Data Sovereignty

Indigenous Data Sovereignty & the Data Shield

⚠ Non-Extractive AI Clause: This archive implements a "Data Shield" — modifications to robots.txt configuration, explicit non-AI-scraping licences embedded in repository root files, and open-source data-licensing scripts protecting indigenous content from commercial AI training. Corporate AI systems are explicitly prohibited from scraping this archive's indigenous knowledge to train Large Language Models without community consent. This represents a new form of digital sovereignty designed to prevent computational colonialism.

🌿
CC BY 4.0 and Indigenous Data Sovereignty — Understanding the Tension

Creative Commons licensing and Indigenous data sovereignty can be in tension. CC BY 4.0 permits broad reuse; Indigenous data sovereignty requires that communities control how their knowledge circulates. This archive resolves the tension as follows:

  • Entries marked as sacred or restricted are held in a non-public layer pending community consultation.
  • First-person community submissions are treated as the intellectual property of the contributor until explicit consent for archiving is given.
  • The CC BY 4.0 licence applies to the project's scholarly apparatus (methodology, architecture, documentation) and to community-contributed data only where consent has been explicitly given.
  • The Data Shield non-scraping clause is an additional contractual restriction layered over the CC licence, protecting indigenous knowledge from AI training use cases not contemplated in traditional open-access frameworks.

For further reading on Creative Commons licensing, visit creativecommons.org.

How to Cite This Archive

Select a citation style below. Click the Copy button to copy instantly to your clipboard. Fill in the access date where indicated with [Day Month Year].

Nandi, Simanta. Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals: A Multi-Layer Community Digital Humanities Archive of Indigenous Food Culture and Cultural Heritage in West Bengal. Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST), 2026, tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n. Accessed [Day Month Year].
💡 MLA tip: Include the access date since this is a digital resource. Use sentence case for the title after the colon.
Nandi, S. (2026). Mapping memory, mapping meals: A multi-layer community digital humanities archive of indigenous food culture and cultural heritage in West Bengal [Digital archive]. Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST). https://tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n
💡 APA 7th tip: The descriptor [Digital archive] in square brackets follows the italicised title. No access date is needed for stable URLs in APA 7th, but check your institutional guidelines.
Nandi, Simanta. "Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals: A Multi-Layer Community Digital Humanities Archive of Indigenous Food Culture and Cultural Heritage in West Bengal." Digital archive. Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST), 2026. https://tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n. Accessed [Day Month Year].
💡 Chicago 17th tip: For website/digital archive citations in Chicago, include both the publication date and the access date.
Nandi, S. (2026) Mapping memory, mapping meals: A multi-layer community digital humanities archive of indigenous food culture and cultural heritage in West Bengal. Digital archive. Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST). Available at: https://tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n (Accessed: [Day Month Year]).
💡 Harvard tip: The access date is essential in Harvard referencing for online sources. Include the full URL.
1. Nandi S. Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals: A multi-layer community digital humanities archive of indigenous food culture and cultural heritage in West Bengal [Internet]. Thiruvananthapuram: Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST); 2026 [cited [Day Month Year]]. Available from: https://tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n
💡 Vancouver tip: Commonly used in medicine and sciences. Numbered sequentially in your reference list. Include [Internet] and [cited date].
Nandi, Simanta. "Mapping Memory, Mapping Meals: A Multi-Layer Community Digital Humanities Archive of Indigenous Food Culture and Cultural Heritage in West Bengal." Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST), 2026. https://tinyurl.com/4ukxfv2n (accessed [Day Month Year]).
💡 Turabian tip: Preferred for academic papers in humanities. Follows Chicago Notes-Bibliography style with minor variations.
📌
Important Notes on Citation
  • This is a living archive — entries are updated monthly. When citing specific food records, note the entry ID (e.g. T-001) and the date of access.
  • The project is licensed under CC BY 4.0 — attribution is required but adaptation is freely permitted.
  • For citing specific food entries: add the entry title and ID after the archive title, e.g. "S. Nandi, Mapping Memory…, Entry T-001: Toto Yashai Nayako (Leaf-Parcel Fish)."
  • For data citations in computational research, use the GitHub repository: github.com/mappingmemorymappingmeals/mappingmemoryproject
